Petz Genes

It's not random.

Introduction

By now, most folks in the Petz community have a good sense of what works — and what doesn't — when it comes to Petz breeding, especially those who've been breeding since the late '90s. But for new and returning players, breeding still proves to be a confusing and often frustrating experience. To this day, even Petz veterans might find themselves scratching their heads when faced with a stubborn pair of parents throwing suspiciously colored offspring.

Fortunately, the vibrant Petz community has pooled together their findings and experiences over the years, shedding some much-needed light on the mystery that's Petz genetics. Unfortunately, many of these community members have moved on, leaving both the community and their research behind. And with the release of new applications and reverse-engineering tools, such as Reflet's GenePoolz, some of these community-standard guides might possibly be considered out-of-date in 2018.

Armed with time-loved Petz breeding guides of the past and modern tools of the present, I decided to see if I could piece together a puzzle that still seemed a bit foggy and unclear. And after working on both a selective breeding project and a purebred-noninbred Dalmatian breeding project, both with fantastic, not to mention fast, results, I thought others in the community might be interested in reading about my experiences and applying what I've learned to their own projects.

Gene Basics

According to GenePoolz, petz carry two sets of genes: eighteen dominant genes and eighteen recessive genes, for a grand total of thirty-six genes making up their complete genetic profile.

When breeding two parents from the adoption center, a mutation may occur. The phrase “genetic mutation” is often used to describe all breeding mutations, mutations being unexpected changes in petz offspring. However, a mutation is not always guaranteed to be a genetic mutation. In fact, the physical appearance of offspring may exhibit a mutation without the offspring carrying the gene in their genetic profile whatsoever. And if a gene is not present in their dominant or recessive gene pools, one or both, the mutation cannot be passed down, ever. Any instances of the gene or exhibited feature appearing to pass down in such a case would in fact be classified as a mutation itself and should not be mistaken as genetics naturally passing down.

Moving forward from here, it’s helpful to imagine petz have two inheritable sets of genes, dominant genes and recessive genes. Based on personal experience, dominant and recessive genes have less to do with genetic profiles of offspring and more to do with their physical appearance. Meaning, parents’ dominant genes are more likely to translate into the exhibited features of offspring than recessive genes. And when two parents share a recessive gene, their offspring has a much higher chance to exhibit that feature than if only one parent carried it.

When genes are passed down from parents to offspring, their genetic profile could map to that of their parents, but often the gene values shuffle. This means that dominant genes can become recessive, and recessive genes can become dominant. Likewise, marking factor values can shuffle from the first position to the second position, and the second position to the first position. Therefore, it’s important to ensure parents share desired genes before breeding to minimize the effects of gene shuffling. That said, unlike genetic mapping and shuffling, genetic mutations are random and cannot be prevented or mitigated.

Finally, it’s helpful to imagine petz have one set of uninheritable, exhibited features, which could be described as their visible appearance. In offspring, exhibited features are normally derived from the genetic profiles of parents—never from the offspring’s own genetic profile—but they have the potential to mutate, just as genes do.

In later stages of breeding projects, parents often carry desired genes in both their dominant and recessive gene pools, which directly affects the likelihood of offspring exhibiting those desired features. Usually the success of a breeding project is measured by how closely offspring resemble the genetic profile they carry, at which point they “breed true,” or as according to the standard of the breeder.

If absolutely nothing else is taken away from this guide, remember that petz genetics indicate only what their children will look like, and any shared characteristics between a petz and their own genetics are entirely coincidental. This is the key to demystifying petz genetics. It’s not random.

GenePoolz Bug Report

The read-me file packaged with GenePoolz reports a possible Petz bug, suggesting that petz may “have” pink (color chart 75) or black (color chart 244) eye color mutations, which, from my understanding, are visibly exhibited but not present in their genetic profile.

However, based on my personal experience breeding and using GenePoolz, I believe this is in fact a feature of Petz, not a bug. As detailed in the section above and throughout the rest of this guide, a petz’ exhibited features may not always reflect their genetic profiles. I argue, and will hopefully demonstrate, this is not limited only to pink or black eyes, but to any and all possible mutations.

Marking Factor

Each genetic profile contains two pairs of marking factor genes, two dominant and two recessive, determining the probability of offspring exhibiting inherited markings and the likely abundancy of these markings. Petz only have room in their genetic profile to carry two groups of markings.

Adoption center parents will always carry marking factor values of 100 (out of 100) in all four gene categories. However, by genetic mutation, second-generation offspring may carry marking factor values anywhere from 0 to 100. Like all mutations, marking factor is not guaranteed to be simultaneously exhibited and carried in offspring's genetic profile.

When breeding, it's possible for offspring to inherit marking factor genes identical to those of the parent, both in value and position. Meaning, if a parent's dominant "Marking 1 Factor" contains a value of 50, their offspring may then inherit a dominant "Marking 1 Factor" value of 50.

However, when offspring inherits a parent’s marking factor genes, neither the value nor the position are guaranteed. Meaning, the marking factor value may jump from “Marking 1 Factor” to “Marking 2 Factor” in offspring. Further, the marking factor value may also jump from dominant to recessive and vice versa. Lastly, a mutated marking factor value may map to the same position, but the value itself may mutate into a new number.

Based on personal experience, marking factor values from 0 to 99 may be more likely to mutate than those at 100, but this is just superstition and could very likely be coincidental mutations. Further, it may also be possible for marking factor values to duplicate and appear more than once in the genetic profiles of offspring. For example, one of my petz carries a marking factor value of 0, but one of their offspring was born with two marking factor values of 0. While this, too, may very well have been a coincidental mutation, I find it a highly improbably one.

Unfortunately, offspring exhibiting low or high marking factor is not a reliable indication they're carriers of inheritable genes. For example, offspring which appear to have no markings may produce offspring with many markings. Likewise, offspring which appear to have many markings may produce offspring with no markings. Again, the source of truth is the genetic profile.

While breeding, take note of which markings are influence by either the “Marking 1 Factor” or “Marking 2 Factor” genes. It’s also important not to dismiss offspring entirely if an ideal marking factor value maps to a less-than-ideal position. With diligent breeding, the gene can be salvaged by the right genetic shuffle.

Coat Colors

Coats can mutate into 10 different colors: black (color chart 35), steel (115), gray (25), white (15), cream (45), dusty (125), sandy (105), ginger (65), cherry (55), and brown (95).

All petz carry two sets of coat color genes, five dominant and five recessive. They are displayed in GenePoolz as an array of colors.

The first color in the array always references a base coat color. For petz who only exhibit one coat color, this is the only color that matters, unless they're parents to mixed-breed offspring, in which case, other colors in the array may be of importance.

Coat Types

Coat Types

When planning desirable markings for breeding projects, don’t mistake coat types as markings, such as the Sheepdog coat type (halfie), Scottie coat type (toes, brows), Poodle coat type (socks), and Dachshund coat type (boots, tail tip).

Sheepdog Coat

Sheepdog coats consist of two coat colors, the base color (first color in the array) and the halfie marking color (second color in the array).

To achieve selfie halfie markings, the first and second colors in the array must match, either by having one parent with matching genes, or one parent carrying the first color gene, and the second parent carrying the second color gene.

Selfie Body Parts

When the colors of body parts match, they are described as “selfie.” “Selfie” body parts may include selfie ears, selfie eyelids, selfie eyes, selfie socks, selfie boots, and so on.

Normally, to be considered “selfie,” only the color itself must match, not necessarily the shade (or color chart number). For instance, any shade of dusty eyelids would be considered “selfie” to a dusty coat. However, the closer the shade is to the coat color, the more effective the look.

It's important to note that cream and steel coats can’t have “selfie” eyelids because eyelids do not mutate into cream or steel colors. The agreed-upon community standard for “selfie” cream coats are dusty, sometimes white, eyelids, and for steel coats, gray eyelids.

Second-Generation Mutations

In second-generation offspring, all genetic mutations will occur in the dominant gene pool and never in the recessive gene pool. Please note, this applies only to marking factor, coat color, eyelid color, and eye color, not body parts, which behave very differently in second-generation offspring, as discussed later.

Because all genetic mutations within second-generation offspring occur in the dominant gene pool, second-generation offspring will never carry recessive marking or color genetic mutations, ever.

As previously covered, second-generation mutations may or may not be exhibited, meaning second-generation offspring may appear identical to, or completely unlike, their first-generation parents while carrying genetic mutations.

Second-Generation Mixed Breeds

If the parents of second-generation offspring are different breeds, meaning the offspring is a mixed breed from two parents from the adoption center, the mother’s body-part genes will be dominant, and the father’s body-part genes will be recessive. Most important, the parents’ body-part genes will never be shuffled in second-generation offspring.

For example, if a first-generation mother is a Dachshund from the adoption center, their offspring’s dominant body-part genes will be made up completely of Dachshund body parts, from head to toe, no matter the breed of the father. Likewise, the offspring’s recessive gene pool will be made up completely of the first-generation father’s body parts. Also keep in mind that because second-generation offspring can’t carry recessive marking or color mutations, their recessive markings and colors will match their father’s, as well. However, the dominant marking and color genes can mutate, despite the mother's genes.

When beginning a breeding project with a specific body part combination in mind, consider which mother and father — which breeds — would be most beneficial to the project, and which breed should make up most dominant body parts.

Remember, second-generation offspring who visibly exhibit a unique combination of body parts cannot carry that genetic profile and will never pass down that combination to third-generation offspring, except by mutation. Body-part genes do not shuffle until the third generation, so don’t waste time breeding a specific combination of uninheritable exhibited features.

Second-Generation Summary

Second-generation offspring have the potential to save or sabotage any breeding project.

Since second-generation marking factor, coat color, eyelid color, and eye color mutations are always dominant, second-generation offspring boast strong genes which may possibly both pass down and be exhibited in third-generation offspring, setting the very foundation for every breeding project.

However, it’s important to remember that the appearance of second-generation offspring cannot always be trusted — only their genetic profile is the source of truth. No matter the appearance of offspring — marking factor, coat color, eyelid color, eye color — the rules above will always apply. Most important, do not be fooled by body parts of second-generation mixed breeds. Body-part genes do not take affect until the third generation, which will be discussed in the next chapter, so don’t waste time breeding second-generation offspring for a specific arrangement body parts.

Before moving forward with any breeding project, it’s critical to first check the genes of potential second-generation parents or risk polluting the gene pool with undesirable genes right from the start.

For best results and ideal third-generation gene profiles, it’s best to breed two second-generation offspring who share desired genes. By breeding second-generation parents with shared desired genetics together, their third-generation offspring has the potential to inherit both parents’ genes, which may fall into both the offspring’s dominant and recessive gene pools. This technique can boost any breeding project, as the genetic advantage gives third-generation parents the most potential to impact the future health and success of the project.

Dalmatian Spots

Within a Dalmatian's genetic profile, the "Marking 1 Factor" value references the Dalmatian's spots, with 0 to 50 being more likely to produce offspring exhibiting low-spot or spotless coats, and 50 to 100 being more likely to produce mid- or high-spot coats.

Dalmatian Ears

Within a Dalmatian's genetic profile, the "Marking 2 Factor" value references the Dalmatian's ears, with 0 to 50 being more likely to produce offspring exhibiting one or two selfie ears, and 50 to 100 being more likely to produce two black ears.

Mutt Patches

First-generation Mutt parents only pass down the patches and ears they exhibit. While the patches and ears may mutate in second-generation offspring, the offspring can only exhibit less patches and ears than their parents, never more and never new. When breeding Mutts, it’s important the parents exhibit the patches desired in offspring, or they will be need to be bred into the line at a later date, which could damage the integrity of the breeding project.
